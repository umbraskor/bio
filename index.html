Ričards Umbraško

Ph.D. Candidate in Slavic Languages and Literatures · Harvard University

I am a Ph.D. candidate in Slavic Languages and Literatures at Harvard University. My research focuses on postcolonial writing from post-Soviet Eastern Europe, media studies and Ukrainian literature and culture, with a particular interest in transnational cultural exchange and archives.

I am a contributing writer for Satori, Latvia's leading literary and cultural magazine, and my other work on Latvian art, society, and politics has appeared in 1843 Magazine (The Economist), New Eastern Europe, and Harvard International Review, among others.

Selected Publications

Dis(b)ordered Lives: Decolonial Temporalities and Borderland Identities in Latvian and Lithuanian Documentary Film
Journal of Global Postcolonial Studies
PDF Link

This article theorizes “border(land) time” through two post-Soviet Baltic borderlands – the eastern Latvian region of Latgale and the Lithuanian nuclear city of Visaginas – as a historically produced, embodied temporality that emerges in the interstices between national and imperial regimes of time and history. Bringing decolonial approaches to temporality into dialogue with post-Soviet cultural studies, the article conceptualizes border(land) time through four temporal dimensions: entanglement, recursion, ritualization, and slowness. In my close readings of two documentary films – Dāvis Sīmanis’s Mūris (D Is for Division, 2018) and Olga Černovaitė’s Drugelio miestas (Butterfly City, 2017) – documentary cinema is treated as a temporal archive that renders lived experiences of time legible. In Latgale, border(land) time is shaped by securitization and anticipatory violence; in Visaginas, it surfaces through nuclear decommissioning and the collapse of Soviet futurity. The article ultimately reframes Baltic borderlands as laboratories of plural temporalities in the long aftermath of empire and under wartime conditions since 2014.

Culture Wars: Latgalian Identity Between Soviet and Latvian Colonial Imaginaries (1958–1959)
Nationalities Papers
PDF Link

By analyzing articles published in the official publication of the Latvian SSR Union of Writers, this article examines how Latgalian identity and culture were constructed by the Soviet Latvian intelligentsia before, during, and after the 1958 Latgale Culture Week in Riga. Interwar-era narratives that had identified Latgale as the Latvian internal Other were endemic to the center-periphery relations in Soviet Latvia during the Khrushchev Thaw. Consequently, politicized representations of Latgale in the late 1950s deferred to the same discursive frames that had contributed to the formation of Latvian national imaginaries of Latgale as underdeveloped, backward, and fundamentally Other. By situating the Culture Week in a colonial setting and critically examining the historical entanglement of Latgale in multiple structures of power – Soviet and Latvian – this article shows that performances of Latgalian identity during the Culture Week became a tool for both nationally minded members of the Latvian Communist Party (LCP) and Russophile Soviet state-builders to consolidate power and project an image of national unity at a time of growing political strife in the LCP.
